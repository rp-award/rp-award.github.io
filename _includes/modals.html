Kun-Hsing Yu

Winner of the 2017 Junior Parasite Award.

The 2017 junior parasite award recipient was Dr. Kun-Hsing Yu. In the research for which he was nominated, Dr. Yu and colleagues employed existing datasets and software in an innovative new analysis. They reanalyzed TCGA histopathology images and Stanford Tissue Microarray data and extracted features using methods built into CellProfiler. They employed a number of different machine learning approaches using packages for the R programming language, which were mentioned and cited in the manuscript. They also provided source code and data for the analyses under an open license.

Dr. Yu says:

I would like to take this opportunity to thank the PSB Parasite Award Committee for organizing this award and my Ph.D. co-advisors Professors Michael Snyder and Russ Altman for supervising my work and nominating me for the award.

Research parasitism, or secondary data analysis, plays a key role in the scientific ecosystem. With data reanalysis, we can ensure the reproducibility of scientific investigations, make the most of the underutilized data, and integrate data from different sources to generate novel biomedical insights. Currently, most biomedical data is trapped in silos, which hinders scientific progress and improvement of healthcare. Biomedical informaticians routinely integrate diverse data types and are in a great position to revolutionize biomedical investigations by breaking the silos, accelerating the scientific process, and translating big data into deep knowledge in biomedicine.

It takes a whole ecosystem to advance science, and secondary data analysis is indispensable for biomedical investigations in the 21st century. As a biomedical informatician, I am very proud of being able to contribute to the ecosystem in a revolutionary way.

Erick Turner

Winner of the 2017 Sustained Parasitism Award.

The 2017 sustained parasite award recipient was Dr. Erick Turner. In the research for which he was nominated [1, 2, 3], Dr. Turner and colleagues identified pervasive publication bias. According to published literature, nearly all clinical trials of antidepressants that they evaluated were positive. However at the FDA level, only half showed a significant positive effect. Dr. Turner and his collaborators have continued to identify reporting biases for a sustained period.

Dr. Turner says:

In each of these studies, I and my colleagues have “parasitically” compared published peer-reviewed journal articles to FDA drug approval packages. These are freely available to the public but unfortunately user-unfriendly. To correct this, we have been developing OpenTrialsFDA, a project aimed at making the FDA’s trove of drug data easier to access and use. The goal is to grow the community of “FDA parasites” so that researchers and others, including journalists, can get a much more complete and “unspun” picture of how safe and effective our drugs really are.

OpenTrialsFDA is currently one of 6 finalists for the Open Science Prize.

Uri Ben-David

Winner of the 2018 Junior Parasite Award.

The 2018 junior parasite award recipient was Dr. Uri Ben-David. In the research for which he was nominated, Dr. Ben-David compiled and analyzed a large collection of public data from patient derived xenografts (PDXs). PDXs are human tumors grown in mice. These are constructed with the hope that they mimic human tumors growing in humans and that they provide a high-fidelity model of human cancers. What these authors found was that these tumors underwent evolution to the mouse environment.

Dr. Ben-David says:

I’m honored and excited to receive the Parasite Award. I’d like to thank the award committee for selecting my work, and – more importantly – for establishing this award to celebrate rigorous secondary analysis of data. Hands down, this is the coolest award I’ve ever received!

n 2018, it’s really quite surprising that we still need to encourage people to re-analyze each other’s data and to view secondary analyses as a positive, and even essential, part of science. My work combines experimental and computational approaches to study cancer genetics, and I can’t think of a single project in our lab that cannot benefit from some secondary data analysis. Simply put, there’s so much published data out there, that it just doesn’t make sense not to use it routinely in our research.

Looking at existing data with fresh eyes is crucial for scientific breakthroughs. I'm proud to be recognized for that sort of achievement, and I intend to keep combining data generation, primary data analysis and secondary data analysis as equally-important aspects of my research.

Julie Dunning Hotopp

Winner of the 2018 Sustained Parasitism Award.

The 2018 sustained parasite award recipient was Dr. Julie Dunning Hotopp. In the research for which she was nominated [1, 2, 3], Dr. Dunning Hotopp and colleagues identified lateral gene transfer between bacteria and animals by examining genome data in public repositories. A continuing theme of Dr. Dunning Hotopp's research has been that there's more to be found by delving into sequences that others have discarded. For her substantial, sustained contributions in this area the committee selected her as the 2018 Sustained Parasitism recipient.

Dr. Dunning Hotopp says:

In these studies, my collaborators, my group, and I have used publicly available genome sequencing data to challenge paradigms, demonstrating that bacterial DNA can integrate into invertebrate animal genomes, and possibly even the somatic human genome. Genome sequencing projects, from the beginning, have embraced the release of raw data to enable testing of alternate hypotheses. Our research program uses such data to demonstrate that not only does bacteria-to-animal lateral gene transfer occur, but that it can be quite common. We continue to develop tools and resources to enable discover of lateral gene transfer in genome sequencing data and continue our own work on bacteria-animal lateral gene transfer.
